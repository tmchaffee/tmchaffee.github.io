Thom's Defense

I defended my PhD dissertation in a public talk at Stanford on Tuesday, May 27th, at 2pm PDT, in person and over Zoom.

The talk both communicated results from my research and celebrated my accomplishments over my five years at Stanford. Here was a brief summary I used to generate interest:

Where would a compass point on the Moon?
Similar to cassette tapes and hard disk drives, the magnetic properties of certain minerals allow rocks to store information for long periods of time. I have studied the magnetically recorded history of the Moon in three different ways: the global pattern of magnetism in the lunar crust as measured by satellites; laboratory studies of rock samples from Apollo 16; and experiments on an unusual lunar metal you've never heard of. I will demonstrate how these experiments and models, as a small piece of a much larger puzzle, tell a magnetic history of the Moon that is much stranger than the Earth's familiar North pole.

I hope you'll be able to join me on the 27th for a brief trip to our planet's closest neighbor.
